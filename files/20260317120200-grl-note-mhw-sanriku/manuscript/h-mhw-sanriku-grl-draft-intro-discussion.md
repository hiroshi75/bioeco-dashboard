## 1. Introduction

Marine heatwaves (MHWs)—prolonged periods of anomalously warm sea surface temperatures (SSTs)—have increased in frequency, intensity, and duration globally over the past century (Oliver et al., 2018). These events cause severe ecological and socioeconomic impacts, including coral bleaching, fisheries collapse, and kelp forest die-offs (Smale et al., 2019). While MHW drivers are often attributed to large-scale climate modes such as the Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) and El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO), the role of mesoscale ocean dynamics—particularly western boundary current variability—remains poorly quantified.

The Sanriku coast of northeastern Japan lies at the confluence of the warm Kuroshio Extension (KE) and the cold Oyashio Current, forming one of the most productive marine ecosystems in the North Pacific. In 2023–2024, this region experienced an unprecedented MHW, with Sugimoto et al. (2025) reporting that the KE reached 40°N for the first time in the satellite era, producing SST anomalies of +4.9°C that persisted for 17 months. However, three critical questions remain unanswered: (1) How does this event compare to historical MHWs under the formal Hobday et al. (2016) classification? (2) What fraction of the SST anomaly is attributable to large-scale climate modes versus KE meander dynamics? (3) What climate regime conditions enabled this extreme event?

Here we address these questions using 43 years of satellite SST observations (NOAA OISST v2.1, 1982–2025), formal MHW detection with a 30-year baseline, and a climate mode decomposition framework. We show that the 2022–2024 Sanriku MHW was a persistent Extreme-category event lasting three years—substantially longer than previously reported—with 60% of the SST anomaly unexplained by PDO, ENSO, and long-term trends. We identify a non-standard PDO response (β_PDO = −0.97, indicating that negative PDO phases promote SST warming in this region) and demonstrate that the event occurred under a climate regime combination (PDO < −1.0 and El Niño) observed only twice since 1950.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Non-standard PDO Response in the Kuroshio-Oyashio Transition Zone

Our finding that negative PDO phases are associated with SST warming off Sanriku (β_PDO = −0.969, p < 0.001) contrasts with the canonical PDO spatial pattern, in which negative PDO produces central North Pacific cooling and modest coastal warming (Mantua et al., 1997). In the KOE transition zone, however, PDO-related SST variability is dominated not by the basin-scale atmospheric bridge but by KE path dynamics. During negative PDO phases, the intensified subarctic gyre destabilizes the KE, promoting large-amplitude meanders that advect warm subtropical waters northward (Qiu & Chen, 2005). This mechanism explains both the reversed sign of β_PDO and the dominance of the KE-related residual (60%) in our decomposition.

The quantitative significance of this finding is substantial: with PDO at −2.77 during summer 2023, the PDO component alone contributed +2.7°C of warming—accounting for the "base" of the anomaly upon which the KE meander superimposed an additional +4.4°C.

### 4.2 Three-Year Persistence and Positive Feedback Mechanisms

The 2022–2024 Sanriku MHW persisted far longer than the 17-month duration reported by Sugimoto et al. (2025), with 2024 exceeding 2023 in both exceedance rate (84.7% vs. 79.4%) and maximum anomaly (+11.2°C vs. +8.7°C). We propose four mechanisms for this persistence:

First, subsurface warm water penetration to 50–400 m depth (Sugimoto et al., 2025) created a deep thermal reservoir resistant to winter surface cooling, enabling year-to-year carryover. Second, the deepening of PDO negative phase from −2.26 (2023) to −2.59 (2024)—the most negative value in 172 years of records—further promoted KE instability. Third, the El Niño–to–La Niña transition during 2023–2024 generated westward-propagating Rossby waves that sustained KE path variability on 1–2 year timescales. Fourth, enhanced air-sea coupling over the warm anomaly (+300 W/m² turbulent heat flux; Sugimoto et al., 2025) created a positive feedback maintaining the warm-core eddies.

The synchronization of MHW termination in early 2025 with a modest PDO recovery (−2.59 to −1.99) further supports the role of PDO phase in controlling the full MHW lifecycle—initiation, persistence, and termination.

### 4.3 Wintertime Extremity and Deep-Ocean Implications

A surprising finding is that the winter anomaly (January 2024, z = 3.99) exceeded the summer anomaly (July 2023, z = 2.68) in standardized terms. While absolute SST anomalies were larger in summer, the lower seasonal variability of winter SST amplified the relative extremity. This wintertime peak is consistent with deep warm-water penetration: winter vertical mixing distributes subsurface warmth throughout the upper ocean, and the enhanced air-sea heat flux maintains the anomaly against radiative cooling. The January 2024 z-score of 3.99 represents an event with an estimated return period exceeding 1,000 years under the 1982–2011 climate, underscoring the truly unprecedented nature of this MHW.

### 4.4 Climate Regime Rarity and Future Risk

The 2023 Sanriku MHW occurred under a climate regime combination—strongly negative PDO (< −1.0) coinciding with El Niño (ONI > 0.5)—that has been observed only twice since 1950 (1972 and 2023). This rarity suggests that conventional PDO-based SST forecasts, which assume linear and stationary relationships, may fail to anticipate extreme MHWs in the KOE region. As long as PDO remains in its current negative phase, the risk of KE meander-driven MHW recurrence remains elevated.

### 4.5 Ecological Implications (brief)

The three-year duration of the Sanriku MHW has profound implications for marine ecosystems. Kelp (Saccharina japonica) harvests in Hokkaido declined to one-third of historical levels by 2024, and warm-water fish species were recorded 500 km northeast of their normal range (Sugimoto et al., 2025). Unlike pulse disturbances from which ecosystems may recover within years, a persistent three-year Extreme MHW may drive irreversible community reorganization, with the extirpation of cold-water foundation species replaced by warm-water colonizers—a pattern described as marine tropicalization (Chust et al., 2024).
