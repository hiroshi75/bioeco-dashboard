# Cover Letter — Seasonal Divergence

Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript "Latitude determines whether climate warming erodes or amplifies seasonal bird community turnover" for consideration as a Letter in Global Change Biology.

**Why this paper matters**: Despite extensive research on annual biodiversity trends, no prior study has examined whether climate warming is reshaping the seasonal structure of ecological communities. Using BioTIME — the largest compilation of long-term abundance time series — we provide the first global analysis of seasonal beta-diversity trends and discover that the direction of change is not uniform but strongly predicted by latitude.

**Key findings**:
- Across 17 long-term bird monitoring datasets (5-39 years, 6 continents), the random-effects pooled estimate of seasonal beta-diversity change was non-significant (p = 0.41, I2 = 82.8%), indicating high between-study heterogeneity rather than uniform global erosion
- Latitude strongly predicts the direction of change (Spearman r = -0.662, p = 0.004, leave-one-out 17/17 robust): high-latitude sites show seasonal erosion (mean = -0.003/yr) while low-latitude sites show seasonal divergence (+0.004/yr)
- This pattern is consistent with asymmetric warming: Arctic amplification drives disproportionate winter warming at high latitudes (seasonal compression), while summer-dominated warming at lower latitudes amplifies seasonal differences
- No prior study has documented trends in seasonal beta-diversity (confirmed by systematic literature search)

**Why GCB**: This work reveals a previously undocumented dimension of biodiversity reorganization under climate change. The finding that global averaging masks predictable, latitude-dependent heterogeneity in seasonal community change has direct implications for biodiversity monitoring frameworks, which currently rely almost exclusively on annual metrics. Our finding complements recent BioTIME analyses of annual turnover (Blowes et al. 2019; Horstmann et al. 2026) by adding the seasonal axis.

**Reproducibility**: All analyses use publicly available BioTIME data. Code and intermediate outputs will be deposited with a Zenodo DOI upon acceptance. A reproducibility issue during analysis (CSV field-name mismatch) was detected by independent verification and resolved through single-pass pipeline rerun, with full documentation available.

This manuscript has not been submitted elsewhere.
