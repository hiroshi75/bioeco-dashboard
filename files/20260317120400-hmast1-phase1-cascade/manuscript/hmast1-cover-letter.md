# Cover Letter — H-MAST-1

Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript "Predicting bear-human conflict from beech mast cycles and summer sunshine: A parsimonious early warning model for Japan" for consideration as a Research Article in the Journal of Applied Ecology.

**Why this paper matters**: Bear-human conflict in Japan reached record levels in 2023 (196+ incidents, 11 fatalities), yet no quantitative predictive model exists. We present a parsimonious 2-variable early warning model that predicts beech mast crop failure — the proximate driver of bear incursions into human settlements — with 78% accuracy (LOOCV, +/-1 category) and 86% recall for severe failures, providing 6 months of lead time for prefectural management.

**Key findings**:
- The previous-year mast score (AR(1) = -1.49) and previous-summer sunshine hours (beta = +0.91) explain 44% of mast variation across 36 years and 5 Tohoku prefectures
- ENSO provides no additional predictive power (delta-AIC = +1.0), demonstrating that local resource cycling and mesoscale meteorology dominate over climate teleconnections
- The 2025 mast crop (score 0.1, severe failure) following the 2024 bumper crop (3.2) triggers an immediate high-risk warning for autumn 2025
- Mast variability has intensified over the study period (CV: 0.55 to 0.92), though this did not cascade to bird communities (6 tests, all null)

**Why JAE**: This work directly addresses the journal's scope of applying ecological science to conservation and management challenges. The model requires no new monitoring infrastructure — only integration of existing Tohoku Forest Office mast surveys and JMA weather data — making it immediately implementable by prefectural authorities.

**Differentiation from prior work**: Kelly et al. (2013) used temperature differentials for European beech masting prediction through correlational analysis without cross-validation. We provide the first F. crenata quantitative prediction system with formal AIC model selection, out-of-sample LOOCV validation, ENSO pathway rejection, and doubled lead time (6 vs 2-3 months).

This manuscript has not been submitted elsewhere.
