# Results Draft — Paper 1 (Sprint 1 里山論文)

## 3.1 Species richness and abundance show no response to environmental change

Across five taxonomic groups (birds, butterflies, frogs, fireflies, plants) monitored at 158 satoyama sites over 15 years, none of the 40 tests relating species richness or abundance trends to environmental change categories (urbanization, cultivation change, forest change, population change) were significant after Benjamini-Hochberg correction (all adjusted p > 0.05; Supplementary Table S1). This comprehensive null result held for all five groups individually: bird species richness showed no association with any environmental driver (Kruskal-Wallis, all p > 0.10), nor did butterfly, frog, firefly, or plant metrics. Shannon diversity index (H') likewise showed no significant difference between abandoned and non-abandoned sites (d = 0.115, p = 0.116). These results confirm that conventional diversity metrics are insensitive to the environmental changes occurring across Japanese satoyama landscapes.

## 3.2 Biotic homogenization: Non-native plant proportions are increasing

In contrast to the null results for species richness, the proportion of non-native plant species (nonnative / [native + nonnative]) showed a significant increasing trend of +0.27% per year (r = 0.717, p = 0.0008), rising from 18.3% in 2008 to 19.7% in 2021. The homogenization index (HI = slope_nonnative − slope_native) was significantly positive (mean HI = +0.012, one-sample t-test p = 0.0005), with 65% of sites showing HI > 0. The ratio-based analysis, which controls for the strong positive correlation between native and non-native trends (r = 0.740), yielded an even stronger effect (d = 0.419, 95% CI [0.143, 0.695], BH-adjusted p < 0.001). Biotic homogenization was not associated with any specific environmental driver (all Kruskal-Wallis p > 0.05), suggesting it is a pervasive phenomenon across satoyama regardless of the dominant land-use trajectory. These results independently confirm the pattern reported by Uchida et al. (2025) using a ratio-based metric that accounts for shared observation effort effects.

## 3.3 Community temperature index analysis: Confounding by baseline temperature

Exploratory CTI analysis using EltonTraits-derived species temperature indices (STI) revealed that abandoned sites had significantly higher CTI than non-abandoned sites (d = 0.497, p < 0.001, n = 644 observations). However, propensity score matching on baseline temperature reduced this effect to non-significance (d = 0.176, p = 0.407, n = 24 matched pairs), indicating that the CTI difference was largely attributable to abandoned sites being located in warmer regions (baseline temperature: abandoned 13.68°C vs. non-abandoned 12.64°C, p = 0.018). The abandonment × temperature trend interaction was not significant (p = 0.90). Mediation analysis in the cold-temperature subgroup (n = 21) showed that forest increase was negatively associated with CTI (r = −0.729, p = 0.003), indicating that afforestation reduces rather than increases thermophilization. These results highlight the importance of controlling for baseline environmental conditions in CTI studies and suggest that apparent thermophilization signals may arise from geographic confounding rather than ecological mechanisms.

## 3.4 Synchronous multi-taxon breakpoints coinciding with climate regime shift

Segmented regression detected significant breakpoints in all eight response variables (all BH-adjusted p < 0.05), clustering into two temporal groups: immediate responders (2008–2010: frogs F = 114.6, butterflies F = 47.2, bird richness F = 47.9, native plants F = 10.4) and delayed responders (2016–2019: fireflies F = 6.0, bird abundance F = 8.2). However, the discrimination test revealed that non-abandoned sites also exhibited significant breakpoints (F = 17.9, p = 0.0002), coinciding with the 2014 Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) phase shift from negative to positive and a +0.55°C annual temperature anomaly increase (JMA data). These breakpoints therefore appear to reflect a climate-driven regime shift affecting all sites rather than an abandonment-specific ecological cascade.
