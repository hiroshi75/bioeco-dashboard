# Cover Letter — RDD Residual Land Paradox

Dear Editor,

We submit "Apparent conservation effects at Japanese protected area boundaries are entirely attributable to residual land placement" for consideration as a Letter in Nature Sustainability.

**Why this paper matters**: The CBD 30x30 target relies on protected areas (PAs) as the primary instrument for biodiversity conservation. Using spatial regression discontinuity design — the gold standard for causal inference at geographic boundaries — we show that apparent biodiversity advantages inside Japanese wildlife sanctuaries vanish entirely after controlling for elevation. PAs harbor cooler bird communities not because of protection but because they occupy higher-elevation residual land unsuitable for development.

**Key findings**:
- Raw CTI was 0.87 C lower inside PAs (p=0.086), suggesting climate buffering
- Progressive elevation control eliminated this difference: latitude proxy (-0.47 C, 46% removed), DEM control (-0.11 C, 87% removed, p=0.45)
- Species richness showed no discontinuity at PA boundaries
- All 4 sites showed systematic BIO1 differences (+2.0 to +4.3 C, inside cooler)

**Why Nature Sustainability**: This finding directly challenges assumptions underlying the 30x30 target. If PAs are predominantly placed on residual lands, expanding coverage without changing placement strategies will not achieve genuine biodiversity protection. Our staged analytical approach provides a replicable template for evaluating PA effectiveness globally.

This manuscript has not been submitted elsewhere.
